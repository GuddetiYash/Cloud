What is Congestion and Contingent Congestion? 

[A] Congestion 

Congestion in a power transmission network occurs when the demand for electricity flow through certain transmission lines or network elements exceeds their designed capacity, reaching or surpassing safe operational limits. This situation prevents additional power flows, which could otherwise lead to overloads. Overloads can result in the overheating of wires and transformers or cause operations to enter unstable states.

[B] Contingent Congestion

Contingent Congestion is a potential increase in congestion that occurs when a specific contingency, such as a line outage or equipment failure, impacts the power supply. You can view and analyze the Contingent Congestion to better understand the implications of various potential contingencies and make more informed decisions to optimize the power performance and reliability.

What is Congestion Analysis 

Congestion Analysis is the identification of congestion and the factors that drive it.

Power flows through elements on the network dependent on injections – generation adds power to the network (positive injection) whereas load takes power from the network (negative injection).

Contingencies model the potential losses of transmission elements on the network.  Facilities have a non-zero probability of failure, and the reliable delivery of power must take this into account.  When elements fail, power will be redirected per the physics of the transmission system across the remaining elements.  These remaining elements must remain below acceptable loading levels should a contingency occur.

The map visualizations and output tabulations help the user to identify when and where congestion occurs on the system, while the congestion analysis tools allow the user to identify how both injections and contingencies impact power flow.

Uses for Congestion Analysis 

All generation owners and load entities are exposed to deliverability risk. Congestion drives price differentials prices between locations (i.e., without congestion, prices would be homogenous), and leads to suboptimal economic dispatch.
 
In regulated markets, congestion leads to higher production costs.  These increased production costs are usually passed on to the rate payer, but the utility has an obligation to reasonably mitigate these costs. In deregulated markets, entities such as asset owners, demand aggregators, and traders need to understand LMP differentials.  Asset owners and demand aggregators will seek to minimize these differentials, whereas traders will want to take an informed position based on these.

  2. Under the Models page, go to the desired Model, and select Run.

Understanding the Different Reports 

[A] Induced Flow Decomposition 

Induced Flow Decomposition details how the different injections (generation or load) at identified buses contribute to the flow on the transmission element being inspected. The characteristics of each injection (capacity, load, and PTDF) are displayed for the specified interval.
 

[B] Congestion Charge Decomposition 

This report allows users to understand the financial impact marginal cost of congestion at each node of interest.  


The LMP of a given node is the sum of the marginal cost of energy, marginal cost of losses, and the marginal cost of congestion.  The congestion charge decomposition report identifies the congestion charge at the given node for each congested element in the system, allowing the user to identify which congested elements are most impactful.
 

[C] Node Flow Report 

The node flow report allows the user to see all flows in and out of a given node.  It reports all injections (generation and demand) as well as detailing flows through lines or transformers, allowing the user to quickly see how power is distributed at that node.
